# Proof Without Logos

## Governing Principle

Never invent named customers, customer logos, metrics, analyst claims, market stats, benchmarks, or quantified outcomes. If proof is not approved or available, build credibility through buyer-relevant specificity, clear use cases, product mechanisms, public category context, and concrete operational examples.

## Use These Alternatives

### 1. Pattern Proof

Describe the common operating pattern the audience recognizes.

Example:
"Healthcare marketing teams often manage acquisition, service-line growth, and provider engagement across disconnected digital touchpoints."

Use when:
The audience shares a known workflow, pressure, or operational challenge.

Avoid:
"Most healthcare marketers..." unless a source supports it.

### 2. Use-Case Proof

Show specific jobs the solution supports.

Example:
"Create separate paths for prospective patients, referring providers, and service-line stakeholders without rebuilding the campaign from scratch."

Use when:
You need credibility without naming customers.

Avoid:
Vague claims like "drive better engagement" with no example of how.

### 3. Mechanism Proof

Explain how the product creates the outcome.

Example:
"Personalized content paths, visitor self-selection, and modular resource hubs help teams match each audience to the next best action."

Use when:
No customer proof is available, but product capability is known.

Avoid:
Outcome claims that the mechanism does not directly support.

### 4. Scenario Proof

Use a realistic, anonymous scenario.

Example:
"Imagine a regional campaign where one visitor is evaluating patient acquisition, another is focused on provider referral growth, and a third needs executive-ready performance visibility."

Use when:
The page needs concreteness but named accounts are not allowed.

Avoid:
Making the scenario sound like a disguised real customer story.

### 5. Public Category Context

Use public, non-customer context from the industry or audience.

Example:
"Healthcare marketers are balancing digital acquisition pressure with rising expectations for relevant, accessible patient experiences."

Use when:
The industry pressure is broadly observable or source-backed.

Avoid:
Specific stats unless cited or provided.

### 6. Resource Proof

Use approved assets, frameworks, checklists, guides, webinars, or product demos as credibility.

Example:
"Explore the readiness checklist to see where your current campaign experience creates friction."

Use when:
The campaign has content assets but no customer logos.

Avoid:
Presenting internal or draft assets as approved public proof.

## Approved Copy Patterns

- "Built for teams managing..."
- "Designed to support workflows like..."
- "Useful when your team needs to..."
- "A practical way to..."
- "Common challenges include..."
- "This approach helps teams..."
- "Use it to compare..."
- "Explore how to..."

## Do Not Use

- Fake customer names or logos.
- Anonymous claims that imply a real customer outcome.
- "Trusted by leading brands" unless approved.
- "Proven to increase..." unless the metric is sourced.
- "Industry-leading" unless approved by brand/legal.
- Any percentage, ranking, benchmark, analyst claim, or market size without a source.
- Internal segment labels, scoring logic, funnel stage names, or account targeting mechanics.

## Final QA

Before final response or publish, verify:

- No invented customer proof.
- No fake logos.
- No unsupported stats.
- No anonymous claim that implies a hidden customer case study.
- Proof is built from audience specificity, use cases, product mechanisms, approved assets, or public category context.
- Any missing proof caveat is surfaced to the user.
